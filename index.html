TEAM BASE CAMPS

Select a camp for restaurant intelligence

Different animal entirely. Fan Zones spike on match days and go quiet between them. Base Camps run hot for three straight weeks. A national team picks a city, moves in with coaches, medical staff, media, security, and a loyal tail of superfans who follow them everywhere. They all eat. Three meals a day, every day, for the entire group stage. Private dining, catering, athlete-grade nutrition, late-night press feeds. If one of these lands in your backyard, you're not dealing with event traffic. You've got a small economy living in your neighborhood. Teams haven't been assigned yet. Selections expected by March 2026. These are the confirmed and projected sites.

HB 1515: “LIBATION” ZONES

Washington's outdoor drinking laws were built for a different era. A million international football fans plaza-hopping with pints wasn't in the playbook. HB 1515 is the state's workaround. It lets cities create temporary "Libation Zones" where alcohol flows outdoors during the tournament. Three tiers, three price tags, all run through the Liquor and Cannabis Board.

But nobody gets to freelance. You don't just roll kegs onto the patio and call it a zone. Your city applies, draws the lines, and files a Joint Operating Plan covering security, cleanup, and minor access. If your municipality is already in the process and you're not talking to them, you're making this harder on yourself later.

Tier 1
Fan Zone Authorization
▼
Exclusive to the 9 designated Fan Zone cities plus Seattle. Valid for events in June and July 2026 only. Allows expanded indoor and outdoor alcohol sales during match viewings. Restaurants within a Fan Zone boundary may extend licensed premises into public right-of-way.
$3,900 per event
Tier 2
Civic Campus Authorization
▼
Available to cities with 220,000+ population: Seattle, Spokane, and Tacoma. Applies to publicly owned civic campuses up to 100 acres. Capped at 25 events per year (max 7 multi-day). Extends through December 31, 2027, enabling post-tournament activations.
$1,500 per event
Tier 3
General Expanded Outdoor
▼
Available to all local governments statewide, including cities, towns, counties, and port authorities. Valid through end of 2027. The catch-all tier that levels the playing field for communities not designated as official Fan Zones. Any municipality can create festive viewing areas with legal outdoor alcohol service.
$1,700 per authorization

Protect Your Labor with Retention Bonuses
1
Revise scheduling before the window opens: overtime thresholds, tip-out structures, emergency call-in protocols. If you're near a Fan Zone or Team Base Camp city, event-industry recruiters will be pulling your best people. Lock them down first.
2
Retention bonuses aren't a perk. They're insurance. Target your highest-risk roles, the ones that hurt worst if they walk. Your TSC can help you identify which positions other operators in your corridor are already protecting.
3
Identify your surge-critical roles (BOH leads, expo, server captains). Write a contingency for each. If one calls out on a match day and your plan is "figure it out," that isn't a plan.
Schedule for SPLH and Update Tip Sharing
1
Your Sales Per Labor Hour targets need to reflect tournament-window revenue, not last summer's averages. Reforecast SPLH by shift for match days, off-days, and the transition gap. If you schedule June the way you scheduled last June, you're either overstaffed on quiet days or underwater on match days.
2
Revisit your tip-sharing structure before the window opens. Extended hours, heavier BOH loads, and international guests who tip differently all put pressure on your current model. Equitable tip pools keep your line cooks and prep team invested when every other kitchen in the corridor is hiring.
3
Build your schedule around the match calendar, not your standard template. A Tuesday with a 4 PM kickoff in Seattle isn't a normal Tuesday. Your TSC can share the full match schedule mapped to projected traffic patterns so you can staff to actual demand.
Staff Training and Compliance Readiness
1
Build daily huddles into the tournament window. Brief. Real-time. What worked, what broke, what changes tomorrow. Teams that feel heard adapt faster. Teams that get ignored quit.
2
Frame this to your crew as elevated season, not just a busy stretch. The difference between "we are slammed" and "we are in the middle of something special" is retention, attitude, and execution quality.
3
Debrief after every major match window, not just at the end. Course-correct while the revenue is still flowing. Your TSC is checking in regularly during the tournament. Use those touchpoints.

Secure and Stockpile Key Items
1
Identify your critical items now, the products that account for 80% of your volume, and talk to your TSC about securing allocations before the tournament window tightens supply. The operators who lock in early have choices. The ones who wait are working with whatever is left.
2
Build a stockpile plan for strategic non-perishables: fryer oil, dry goods, paper products, cleaning supplies. Evaluate rented refrigerated trailers or portable dry-storage units for the 5-6 week window. Your Harbor Restaurant Solutions Advisor can help model storage needs against your projected volume.
3
Pre-book beer and wine allocations from your distributor. Premium domestic and imported lagers will be constrained during group stage. If you wait for the first "out of stock" notification, you're already scrambling. Your TSC knows which products are already showing tightness in your corridor.
Protect Your Supply Chain
1
Schedule a strategy call with your TSC now. Not in May. Now. They know your delivery corridor, your critical items, and where the pressure points are. Get your delivery schedule adjusted before the window narrows, not after your first missed drop.
2
Night drops are your best friend during the tournament. Daytime delivery windows into Seattle and the I-5 corridor will be unreliable on match days. Work with your TSC to shift to overnight receiving where your operation allows it.
3
Build product substitution protocols now for your top 10 items. If your primary cut or brand is unavailable, what's the backup? Having that decision made in advance means your kitchen doesn't stall while someone makes a phone call.
Alternative High-Throughput Menus
1
Design a Fast Track menu: 8-12 items, every one plates in under 4 minutes, zero tableside prep. This isn't a dumbed-down menu. It's a surgical one. Your best items, built for speed.
2
Build a Locals menu that gives your regulars status. Preferred items, pricing acknowledgment, priority access. Your regulars are your brand continuity. Do not let the tournament erode that relationship. They're coming back in August. The tourists are not.
3
If you have outdoor space, activate it. Even a temporary beer garden with a simplified menu can double effective capacity during good weather. Check your HB 1515 eligibility and talk to your municipality about outdoor service boundaries.

01
HB 1515: Libation Zones
▼
What It Is
HB 1515 is state legislation that allows cities to create temporary "Libation Zones" where alcohol can be served outdoors in public spaces during the tournament window. This isn't a blanket deregulation. It's a permitting framework that your municipality applies for and administers. You don't get to declare your own zone. Your city draws the lines, files a Joint Operating Plan, and runs it through the Liquor and Cannabis Board.
The Three Tiers
Tier 1: Fan Zone Authorization. Exclusive to the 9 designated Fan Zone cities plus Seattle. Valid June and July 2026 only. Allows expanded indoor and outdoor alcohol sales during match viewings. Restaurants within a Fan Zone boundary may extend licensed premises into public right-of-way. Fee: $3,900 per event. Borne by the applicant (city or organizing entity), though costs may be passed to vendors through booth fees or revenue sharing.
Tier 2: Civic Campus Authorization. Available to cities with 220,000+ population: Seattle, Spokane, and Tacoma. Applies to publicly owned civic campuses up to 100 acres. Capped at 25 events per year (max 7 multi-day). Extends through December 31, 2027, enabling post-tournament activations. Fee: $1,500 per event.
Tier 3: General Expanded Outdoor. Available to all local governments statewide: cities, towns, counties, port authorities. Valid through end of 2027. The catch-all tier that levels the playing field for communities not designated as official Fan Zones. Any municipality can create festive viewing areas with legal outdoor alcohol service. Fee: $1,700 per authorization.
The Joint Operating Plan
Every jurisdiction applying for Libation Zone authorization must submit a Joint Operating Plan to the LCB. This document covers security protocols, litter removal, enforcement mechanisms for over-service, and minor access prevention. If your restaurant participates in a zone, you will likely sign an agreement adhering to this plan. That may include standardized compostable cups, shared security fund contributions, and strict last-call times synchronized with match endings.
What You Need to Do
Contact your municipality now to determine if they're pursuing HB 1515 authorization and which tier applies to your location. If you're inside or adjacent to a designated zone, ask about the vendor participation process and timeline. You can't retroactively join a zone after it activates.
If you have outdoor space you want to activate, the permitting conversation must start with your city, not the LCB. Your city applies; you participate within their framework. Your TSC can confirm which Fan Zone cities are actively pursuing authorization.
Sunset Clause
These provisions are temporary. Tier 1 expires after July 2026. Tiers 2 and 3 expire December 31, 2027. The legislature will review impact data in January 2027 to determine whether to make any provisions permanent. Operators who collect and report sales data during the tournament actively support the case for permanent Libation Zone legislation.
02
LCB Compliance and Liquor Enforcement
▼
The Enforcement Reality
The Liquor and Cannabis Board will increase enforcement activity during the tournament window. This isn't speculation. High-profile international events draw additional compliance checks as a matter of standard protocol. If your operation has deferred maintenance on any liquor compliance item, the tournament is the worst possible time for that to surface.
Hours of Sale
Standard Washington hours-of-sale laws still apply unless your municipality has secured expanded-hours authorization through HB 1515. If you aren't inside an authorized zone, your last-call time doesn't change because there's a match on. Serving after hours during a tournament carries the same penalties as serving after hours on any other night: worse, because enforcement will be watching.
If your municipality is pursuing expanded hours within a Libation Zone, confirm the exact parameters. "Expanded" doesn't mean "unlimited." Hours will be tied to match schedules and zone-specific operating plans. Know your window before you staff for it.
Signage and Premises Audit
Your liquor license must be posted and visible. Required signage (minor prohibition, hours of sale, responsible service notices) must be current. If you have expanded into outdoor service areas under HB 1515, those areas are now part of your licensed premises and must meet the same signage requirements as your interior.
Walk your premises now with compliance eyes. Anything that looks temporary, improvised, or jury-rigged will draw attention during an enforcement check. If you're adding screens, outdoor seating, or temporary bars for the tournament, build them to the same standard as your permanent fixtures.
Over-Service and Minor Access
Visibly intoxicated service violations carry the same weight during a tournament as any other day, but the volume of intoxicated patrons will be meaningfully higher. Match-day crowds, especially post-victory and post-elimination, will push your staff's judgment on over-service calls. Reinforce your cut-off protocols before June.
Minor access prevention requires extra vigilance with international guests. A 20-year-old from a country where the drinking age is 18 won't understand why they're being refused. Your staff needs to be prepared for that conversation before it happens, not during a rush.
TSC Action
Your TSC can walk through a pre-tournament LCB compliance checklist specific to your license type and location. If your operation is inside a Fan Zone, this conversation should happen before May.
03
Safe Marketing and FIFA Clean Zones
▼
Clean Zone Restrictions
FIFA designates "Clean Zones" around stadiums and official Fan Zones. Inside these zones, only official FIFA sponsors (Visa, Coca-Cola, etc.) can have visible branding. If your restaurant is within a Clean Zone boundary, non-sponsor branding visible from the street may need to be covered or removed during the tournament window. A banner for a local non-sponsor brewery in your window could be a violation.
The Clean Zone boundary for Seattle's Lumen Field area and Seattle Center will be published. If you operate in Pioneer Square, SODO, CID, or Lower Queen Anne, get clarity on whether your frontage falls inside the line. Neighborhood Liaisons are available for permit and access questions in those corridors.
Prohibited Terms
You can't use the following in any marketing, signage, menus, social media, or promotional materials: "World Cup," "FIFA," "FIFA World Cup 2026," "Seattle 26," the FIFA trophy image, or any official tournament logos or fonts. Naming a dish "The World Cup Burger" is a trademark violation. Using the tournament logo on a flyer is a trademark violation. FIFA enforces this aggressively and has done so at every prior tournament.
Permitted Language
Use these instead: "Summer of Soccer," "International Football," "The Big Tournament," "The Beautiful Game." These are generic terms that signal participation without triggering trademark enforcement. Visit Seattle's Community Brand Playbook provides safe visual assets ("Let's Play SEA 26" graphics) that restaurants can use to signal participation without legal risk.
Watch Party Licensing
Non-Commercial (allowed): Bars and restaurants can show matches on their TVs as part of normal business operations provided they don't charge an admission fee. No special FIFA license required. This is the viable path for 99% of operators.
Commercial (restricted): If you charge entry, sell event sponsorship, or brand it as a "FIFA" event, you must apply for a FIFA Public Viewing License. This involves fees, strict reporting requirements, and branding constraints. Unless you have a specific reason and legal counsel, avoid this path.
The Simple Rule
Keep entry free. Monetize through food and beverage. Use generic language. Do not put FIFA's name on anything. Your TSC has a cheat sheet of safe terminology. Ask for it before you print anything.
04
International ID Verification and Staff Training
▼
The Problem
Your bartenders and servers will be asked to verify the age of guests carrying passports from 48 different countries. Most of your staff have never seen a Moroccan national ID, a Brazilian CPF card, or an Algerian passport. The legal obligation doesn't change because the document is unfamiliar. If your 19-year-old server can't confidently verify a foreign ID, you have a compliance gap that the LCB won't excuse because the tournament was busy.
Acceptable Identification
Valid for age verification in Washington: Current passport (any country), Washington State ID or driver's license, other US state ID or driver's license, US military ID. A foreign driver's license alone is generally not sufficient unless accompanied by a passport.
Train staff on the default protocol: if the ID is unfamiliar, request the passport. Every international visitor will have one. Make it policy, not a judgment call. Consistent application protects both the guest and your license.
Staff Training Before June
Run a passport verification session with every front-of-house employee who handles alcohol sales. Show them what a passport age page looks like, where the date of birth is located, and how to calculate age across date formats (DD/MM/YYYY is standard internationally, not MM/DD/YYYY). One training session in May prevents a compliance violation in June.
Post a quick-reference card at every POS station and behind every bar: "When in doubt, ask for the passport. Date of birth format: DD/MM/YYYY." Visual cues reduce errors under pressure.
Human Trafficking Awareness
State agencies and the Local Organizing Committee are emphasizing social responsibility during the tournament. Businesses Ending Slavery and Trafficking (BEST) provides specialized training for hospitality staff to recognize and report signs of trafficking. Completing this training isn't currently mandated for all operators, but it's a component of being a preferred partner or vendor for official events. It's the right thing to do, and it positions your operation as a responsible community partner.
The Date Format That Will Trip You Up
A passport showing 03/07/2005 means July 3, 2005 internationally, not March 7. That guest turns 21 on July 3, 2026, not March 7. If your bartender reads it the American way, they serve a 20-year-old in June. One training session fixes this.
